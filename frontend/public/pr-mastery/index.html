BECOME THE EXPERT

PR 101 — everything no one told you.

Read this once and you'll understand more about how media actually works than most people who claim to "do PR." This is the foundation that makes the rest make sense.

What PR actually is

Public Relations is earning attention and credibility through other people — journalists, podcast hosts, conference stages, industry awards — instead of buying it.

The core trade is this: you give up control of the message in exchange for credibility. An ad you write yourself is 100% on-message but everyone knows you paid for it. A journalist at Forbes writing that you're a leader in your space? You can't fully control what they say — but it's worth ten times more, because the audience trusts them, not you.

That single sentence — "you trade control for credibility" — is the most important thing on this page. It explains why PR is hard, why it can't be guaranteed, and why it's worth real money.

The map: the PESO model

Every kind of media falls into four buckets. Knowing this instantly makes you sound fluent:

  • P — Paid: Ads. You pay, you control the message. (Facebook ads, TV spots, sponsored posts.)
  • E — Earned: Coverage a journalist gives you because the story is worth telling. This is the heart of PR — and it's what Story Group sells.
  • S — Shared: Social media — your posts, reposts, engagement.
  • O — Owned: Channels you control — your website, blog, newsletter. (Bylines live at the edge of earned + owned.)

When a prospect says "we already do marketing," they almost always mean Paid + Shared + Owned. The gap you fill is Earned — the one bucket you can't buy your way into.

How earned media really happens

Coverage isn't magic and it isn't a press-release blast. It's a pipeline. Knowing it lets you talk process like a pro:

  • 1. The angle (news hook): Decide what story is actually worth telling — tied to something happening now.
  • 2. The media list: Identify the specific journalists who cover that exact beat. Not a database dump — the right 30–75 names.
  • 3. The pitch: A short, personalized email to one journalist proposing the story (not "please cover us").
  • 4. The follow-up: Most pitches get ignored. Human, persistent follow-up is where placements are actually won.
  • 5. The placement: The journalist writes the story or quotes your exec. That's "earned coverage" — a hit, a clip.
  • 6. Amplification: You share the coverage (owned + shared) to multiply its value.

When a prospect asks "so what do you actually do all month?" — this pipeline, on repeat, across multiple storylines, is the answer.

What journalists actually want

Journalists don't care that you're great. They care about newsworthiness. A pitch lands when it has one of these:

  • Timeliness — it ties to something in the news right now.
  • Data or exclusivity — a new survey, original numbers, a story they'd be first to report.
  • A trend they're already covering — and you're the perfect example or expert source.
  • A real human story — conflict, transformation, an underdog.

"We're excited to announce…" is not news. This is exactly why companies need a strategist — finding the hook is the skill they're paying for.

The assets, explained simply

Press release

An official announcement (funding, launch, big hire) written and distributed to outlets. Useful, but the weakest tool alone.

Media pitch

A personalized email to one journalist proposing a story. Far more effective than a mass release.

Byline / contributed article

An article published under your exec's name (e.g. a Forbes council piece). Pure thought leadership.

Op-ed

An opinion piece arguing a position, run in a news outlet. Positions your leader as a voice with a point of view.

Earned interview / quote

A journalist quotes your exec inside their own story. The classic "we got mentioned in…" win.

Podcast placement

Booking your exec as a guest on relevant shows. Long-form trust-building at scale.

Speaking placement

Getting your leader on a conference stage or panel. Authority you can't fake.

Awards

Industry award submissions for third-party validation. Cheap credibility.

Campaigns worth name-dropping

A few legendary campaigns. Reference one and you instantly sound like you live in this world:

  • Dove "Real Beauty" (2004) — sparked by a finding that only 2% of women felt beautiful; used real women, not models. Sales went from $2.5B to $4B+ in a decade. "It's not about the soap — it's about what Dove stands for."
  • IKEA / Mark Malkoff — a comedian "lived inside an IKEA store" and filmed it. Sold the feeling of home, not products — won Campaign of the Year.
  • KFC, COVID — blurred out "Finger Lickin'" from its slogan and defused backlash with humor. Read the room, respond with wit.
  • Newsjacking (Christina Daves) — clients drove 873M views and 8-figure sales from free press by tying their expertise to what's in the news right now (a skincare chemist pitching DIY sanitizer when shelves emptied).

How PR is measured today

If a prospect asks about ROI, here's the modern, honest answer — and saying it correctly is an instant credibility signal:

  • Quantity & quality of placements — how many, in which outlets (a trade vs. Bloomberg are not equal).
  • Share of Voice (SOV) — how much of the conversation in your category is about you vs. competitors.
  • Sentiment — is the coverage positive, neutral, negative.
  • Branded search lift & referral traffic — are more people Googling you and clicking through.
  • Inbound — speaking invites, partnership asks, leads that say "I saw you in…"

Red flag to avoid: AVE (Advertising Value Equivalency) — the old "this coverage = $X of ads" math. It's been discredited industry-wide. Never use it. Story Group tracks the real metrics above through Meltwater.

How long it actually takes

PR is a compounding asset, not a faucet. Set this expectation upfront and you'll never get the "nothing happened in month 1" complaint:

  • Weeks 1–2: Research, narrative build, media list, first outreach prep. (Tell them this happens fast — outreach begins within 2 weeks of kickoff.)
  • Weeks 4–12: First placements typically land. Momentum starts.
  • Months 6–12: Authority compounds — journalists start coming to you.

What PR can and can't do

It CAN

  • Build credibility and authority
  • Grow share of voice vs. competitors
  • Drive inbound — leads, partners, speaking
  • Support fundraising, recruiting, and sales
  • Manage and protect reputation

It CANNOT

  • Guarantee a specific placement (promising this is a scam tell)
  • Deliver overnight results
  • Fix a genuinely bad product
  • Control exactly what a journalist writes

The offer — four levels to know cold.

This is your product knowledge and your Call 2 toolkit. Know every deliverable so you never fumble — but remember the Price Rule: on Call 1 you band the budget and present none of this. Tiers, prices, and the spectrum walk all happen on Call 2.

The Price Rule: On Call 1, never name a tier or quote a tier price. Band only — "$5–10k, $10–20k, or higher?" Everything below is for Call 2 and for your own command of the product.

The real jumps, in plain English

Foundation → Amplify: passive to active

Foundation is a press release and you wait. Amplify is a plan, a real media list, and someone persistently working storylines every month. Foundation is a press release. Amplify is a media strategy.

Amplify → Influence: coverage to voice

This is the jump most prospects debate. At Amplify a journalist writes a story and might include your data point. At Influence, that journalist already knows your CEO's name and calls them for a quote before publishing. Amplify is PUSH (you pitch outward). Influence is PULL (they come to you). Bylines, op-eds, podcasts, and proactive booking create that pull.

Influence → Command: voice to armor

At Influence you're building authority. At Command you're also managing risk — media coaching before every appearance, crisis holding statements ready, board-ready analytics. For people where a bad headline costs millions.

The 3 things at $15K you don't get at $8.5K

  • Higher-impact assets: $8.5K gets pitches and releases. $15K gets bylines, op-eds, and podcast appearances — content that makes your leader a thought leader, not just a news source.
  • Proactive booking as a core pillar: $8.5K booking is opportunistic. $15K, you're actively and continuously booked. The difference between hoping a journalist calls and making sure they do.
  • Meltwater intelligence: Share-of-voice, sentiment, competitor monitoring. Enterprise tools that cost $12–30K/yr alone — baked in. Lets you prove ROI to a board.

The call — discovery, not pitch.

Story Group's real Call 1 script (v6, May 2026) — the first of a two-call close. Call 1 qualifies and extracts; Call 2 pitches and closes. It's problem-first: you open with pain, never with goals. 30 minutes, run with discipline. Italic = say it close to verbatim.

The 3 non-negotiable rules

1 · Cole Gordon

Tell them up front there's a second call. No surprises, and never pivot discovery into a pitch inside Call 1.

2 · The Price Rule

Never name a tier or quote a tier price on Call 1. Band the budget: $5–10k / $10–20k / higher. Tiers come on Call 2.

3 · Pure listening

Through Beat 6: no coaching, no reframes, no teaching. Extraction first. All coaching lives in the close.

A 30-second exchange. Match their rhythm but lead with your own grounded energy — don't mirror theirs. Don't linger.

2

Leadership Move · 0:30–1:00

"Great. I have a limited amount of time — are we ready to jump in? Do you have something to take notes with?"

Sub-communicates discipline, opens the loop, and forces them to grab a pen.

The Frame — Cole Gordon Call 2 commit · 1:00–2:00

"Everything we do is customized based on what people need. So we're going to get clarity on what you need, and I'll tell you whether this is something we can help with. If it's a fit, I'll take what we discussed back to our team, we'll build out a customized game plan, and we'll book another call where I'll walk you through exactly how it's going to help you. Does that make sense?"

Wait for an explicit yes. Don't move on until they acknowledge.

4

Case Study Opener · 2:00–3:30

"Before we get into your situation — what caught my eye is [STRUCTURAL OBSERVATION]. We just helped [MATCHED CASE STUDY] [TWO REAL METRICS]. Made me think there's a version of that we could run for you. Sound fair?"

A credibility drop before discovery. Slots: a one-sentence observation of what they DID · one anonymized case from your library (see Case Studies) · two real metrics — never fabricate.

The 7 Beats (3:30–24:00)

The rule for every beat: ask, then shut up. 2–4 seconds of silence — the real answer lives in the pause. Write their punchy phrases verbatim. Pure listening through Beat 6.

1

Pain / Background · 3:30–7:00

"Walk me through what brought you to this call. What's going on in [company / practice / campaign] right now that made you want to have this conversation?"

[Silence — count to four.] Then "Got it. Anything else?" — ask up to 3×. Stop the moment they settle, repeat themselves, or say "that's really it." Forcing a 3rd ask on someone who's settled reads as inattention.

Then deploy your pattern read (see "Read the prospect" below) — but only after they've settled.
2

Specific Examples · 7:00–11:00

"You said [their #1 pain, verbatim]. Give me a specific example — not a generic one, the most recent time it showed up. Walk me through it." … "And one more. What's another specific time it bit you?"

Chunk down by the type of metric they give:

If they can't produce a specific example, the pain isn't real yet — loop back to Beat 1.
3

Money / DM / Banded Budget · 11:00–14:00

"Quick gear-change — two questions. First: besides yourself, who else weighs in on a decision like this — a partner, co-founder, board, CFO? Second: have you set aside a budget for this, or are we still in the figuring-it-out phase?"
"Roughly what range are you thinking — is this a $5–10k a month conversation, $10–20k, or are we higher than that?"

Price Rule: banded only. Never name our floor, never name a tier, never quote a tier price.

Pushback: "Totally fair — here's why I ask. The work at $5k looks different from the work at $20k. I'm not pinning you to a number, I'm figuring out which conversation we're having." If they say "just tell me what it costs": "Fair — and I will, on the pitch call once I know what to recommend. Today I just need to know if we're in the same ballpark: $5–10, $10–20, or higher?"

4

How Long / What Tried · 14:00–17:00

"How long has this been a problem? When did you first notice it was something you needed to actually solve, not just live with?" … "And in that time, what have you tried? PR firm, freelancer, in-house, agency? [for each] What happened — why didn't it work?"
"What's your own theory of why it didn't work?"
The single most valuable line in the call. Their theory of past failure shapes the Call 2 pitch ("no hustle" → lead with hustle; "no strategy" → lead with strategy). Capture verbatim. Also flag: incumbent-vendor risk and the burned signal (every Call 2 claim then needs a proof point).
5

Why Now + Cost of Inaction · 17:00–20:00

"You've had this for [duration]. Why are we talking now? What changed in the last 30 to 60 days that made you take this call this week?"
"If nothing changes — if you do nothing differently for the next 6 to 12 months — what does that cost you? Not just dollars. Reputation, opportunity, the deal you don't get, the seat you don't earn."

[Silence. Sit with it. Don't rescue. Count to six.] Then the double-pump:

"Is there any other reason this matters to you?"
First answer = rational (revenue, market share). The second answer, after the double-pump, is emotional ("the board is losing confidence," "my wife is tired of me bringing this home"). That second answer closes Call 2.
6

Goal + NMG · 20:00–24:00

"Flip the coin. Twelve months from now, you look me in the eye and say 'that worked.' What's specifically different in the business?" … "When you say [their answer], what does that cash out as — inbound leads, board confidence, donor meetings, speaking invites, investor interest, votes?"
"I'm going to ask you something a little personal — tell me to back off if it's not appropriate. Mind if I ask? … If that outcome happens, what does it mean for YOU personally? Not the company. You."
This is the NMG (non-monetary goal) — board credibility, peace of mind, freedom from the founder seat, a clean exit, a spouse who stops worrying. Capture the exact words. It closes Call 2.

Transition + Close (24:00–30:00)

Extraction is done. Now coaching, reframes, and the close are allowed.

7a

Optional Thought Drop · binary trigger

Drop this only if you can name a real client + real industry match + real result in one sentence each. Any doubt → skip it. A weak case study does more damage than none.

"About [timeframe] ago we worked with a client almost identical to what you described — [one sentence]. What we did was [one sentence]. The outcome was [one specific result]. Not pitching you on that approach today — just sharing the pattern."
7b

Tie-down → Trial Commit → Partner → Call 2 → Loom · 27:00–30:00

Tie-down:

"Here's where we are. I've got a clear picture of [pain in their words], the fact that [trigger], and what you're trying to get to — [goal], specifically [NMG]. Did I get that right?"

Trial commit:

"Before I put time into building a tailored plan — if we bring you a plan that hits [their goal], is this something you're ready to make a real decision on in the next two weeks?"

Partner (if a DM surfaced in Beat 3): "You mentioned [name/role] weighs in. The plan will be specific enough that having them on Call 2 saves a round trip. Any chance we get them on directly?" — DM on the call is best; committed loop-in within 48h is acceptable; "I'll mention it after" is not.

Call 2 preview + named outlet: "Call 2 is 45–60 min — I'll walk you through what we'd do month by month, including which outlets we'd target. Based on what you told me, I personally believe [ONE named outlet] is on the table. Not promising it today, but I'll come with a real point of view." Only name an outlet you genuinely believe is achievable — your credibility is on it.

Loom: "Between now and then I'll record a 90-second Loom walking through what stood out. It keeps us on the same page going into Call 2. Sound good?" — get the verbal yes.

Read the prospect before you dial

Par-to-Excellence (most PR buyers)

Doing fine, watching opportunity pass. Pain = the gap to the next level. Use the two-truths move: "What you've built is real — and you're here because you can see the next level and you're not there yet."

Sub-Par-to-Par (crisis)

Something is on fire — lawsuit, bad coverage. Posture: calm, fast, clinical. Do NOT use two-truths. "I hear you. Let's figure out what we're actually dealing with."

Burned / Skeptical

A prior firm failed them. Receipt language only — every Call 2 claim needs a proof point. "Fair concern. Here's how we work differently, and you can verify it before you sign."

Disqualify + book (live scripts)

D-10 · They hedge on the trial commit+

"Fair answer — appreciate the honesty. Help me understand what would make you ready? A piece of info you'd need, a person you'd need to talk to, a threshold in the business — what's the actual thing?" [Listen — the real objection surfaces here.] "So if we addressed [their answer] on the next call, you'd be in a position to say yes? Then I'll build the plan with [their answer] specifically addressed — so our next call is either 'yes, let's go' or 'no, here's exactly why not.' Fair?"

Never accept a vague exit. Pin the specific decision criterion or disqualify.

D-11 · Disqualify kindly (budget fail)+

"I'll be just as straight as you were. The work we do starts at $5k a month on a four-month commitment, so at our level we're not the right partner right now — and I'd rather tell you that than run you through a pitch that isn't aligned. Two things: first, when you're ready — a quarter, a year, whenever — call me directly. Second, a favor, not a pitch: know anyone where a real PR budget makes sense? We pay $500 if a referral signs."

Floor rule: naming the $5k / 4-month floor is permitted here only — this is a disqualification + referral conversation, not a sale. The floor stays off the table everywhere else on Call 1.

D-12 · Disqualify kindly (no DM access)+

"Honest take: when the actual decision-maker isn't in the room, nobody wins — you play telephone, they have questions you can't answer, and the deal stalls or dies for the wrong reasons. So let's not book the pitch yet. Go have a 10-minute chat with [DM] this week — if they're open to a 45-minute call, email me and we'll get all three of us on the calendar. If not, we let it go for now and you have an ally here whenever timing changes. Fair?"

Refusing to pitch without the DM is the only path that wins. A gatekeeper-only pitch is the most expensive mistake we make.

D-13 · Book Call 2 (on the line)+

"Two times that make sense — I want to give you the plan while this is fresh. [Day] at [Time], or [Day] at [Time]?" … "Done — invite going out now. Confirm [partner / DM] is on the invite too?"

Call 2 within 4–7 days. Beyond that, emotional openness fades. Add attendees while you're both still on the line.

BEFORE YOU DIAL

Call prep — walk in loaded.

Twenty minutes of prep is the difference between a generic call and one where the prospect feels you already get them. Do this before every Call 1.

Research the prospect (run in parallel)

  • LinkedIn / background — title, tenure, prior roles, credentials, thought-leadership surface area.
  • Company website — what they do, size signals, positioning, any press/news page.
  • Google News — 2–4 real articles with outlet + date + URL. If there's no coverage, that's the trigger.
  • Competitor press — 1–3 direct competitors with specific articles where they got coverage (URLs, dates).
  • Funding / revenue signals — Crunchbase, filings, employee count.

Every URL must be real and verified. Never fabricate.

Estimate the revenue (Mode A or B)

Mode A · Public economics

Pricing pages, tickets, fees, published ARR → closed pre-call math. You walk in already knowing the number.

Mode B · Private economics

Patients/day, customer count, LTV, conversion → industry benchmark + a live-fill formula you complete during Beat 5.

Pick the prospect pattern

Pre-load one of three reads so your Beat 1 response is ready: Par-to-Excellence (most buyers — use two-truths), Sub-Par-to-Par (crisis — calm and clinical, no two-truths), or Burned/Skeptical (receipts only). Full descriptions live in The Call.

PR in the AI era — where buyers search now.

Your prospects' customers increasingly ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's AI — not just the blue links. And the #1 thing that decides whether a brand shows up in those answers is earned media. Almost no other PR seller can explain this. After this page, you can — and it might be your single sharpest pitch.

84%

of what AI tools cite comes from earned media — not brand websites or blogs. (Muck Rack, 2026 analysis of 25M+ AI citations.)

~48%

Scale check: ChatGPT serves ~900M weekly users and 2.5B+ prompts a day (2026) — and brands with consistent earned media get cited several times more often than those leaning on their own blog. This isn't a fringe channel.

UNDERSTAND WHAT'S PRODUCED

The craft — what good looks like.

You'll never have to write these yourself — but understanding them is what lets you talk shop with a CEO and explain, concretely, what their retainer actually buys.

Anatomy of a pitch

The pitch is the core unit of PR — a personal email to one journalist. The reality of their inbox is brutal, which is exactly why skill matters:

  • 86% of journalists immediately reject pitches that aren't on their beat. Top delete reasons: irrelevant (86%), too promotional (71%), mass-email feel (50%), too long (43%).
  • 69% want pitches under 200 words · 62% prefer a 1:1 email · 78% want them before noon · one follow-up after 3–7 days is fine.
  • 84% of journalists say at least some of their stories start from a PR pitch — but only the on-beat ones land. Write to ONE reporter, ONE beat, ONE hook.
  • Subject line: 6–10 words, specific, curiosity + relevance. Never start with "Press Release," "Breaking," "Urgent," or "[Company] announces."
Muck Rack, State of Journalism 2025 · Cision, State of the Media 2025
Subject: New data: 60% of clinics still fax patient records

Hi Sarah — Saw your piece on healthcare's tech lag. We just pulled data from 400 clinics showing most still rely on fax. Three findings I think your readers would want:
• 60% fax records weekly
• It adds 2.3 days to referrals
• The fix costs less than one staffer
Our CEO (ex-Epic) can walk you through it this week. Worth a look?

Notice: their story first, real data, a clear human ask — not "we're excited to announce."

Anatomy of a press release

  • Headline — the news in one line. Subhead — the so-what.
  • Dateline — CITY, State, Date — then the lede: who/what/when/where/why in 1–2 sentences.
  • Body — context, a quote from leadership, supporting data.
  • Boilerplate — the standard "About [Company]" paragraph.
  • Media contact — name, email, phone.

A release is for the record, the wire, and SEO. It rarely lands a story by itself — the personalized pitch does that. Knowing the difference is a pro tell.

Anatomy of a byline / op-ed

Ghostwritten under your exec's name. This is how authority is manufactured — legitimately.

  • A real point of view tied to a current trend — not a product ad.
  • 3 supporting arguments, each with evidence or a story.
  • A forward-looking close that positions the author as a thinker.
  • Homes: Forbes / Fast Company / Entrepreneur councils, Harvard Business Review, and industry trades.

How quotes actually happen (sourcing tools)

When you hear "our CEO got quoted in Forbes overnight," this is often how — journalists post requests, experts respond fast:

  • Qwoted — verified journalists + experts; 70% of requests come from DR 80+ publications (Forbes, Reuters, CNBC, WSJ). The serious one.
  • Featured (the relaunched HARO) — free daily digests of reporter queries. (The original HARO/Connectively shut down; Featured.com revived it in 2025.)
  • Source of Sources (SOS) — free, honor-system queries from HARO's original creator.

Stay on message — media training

When you place an exec, this is what keeps the journalist from steering them somewhere they don't want to go. Three techniques (PRLab):

  • Soundbite — a 10–20 second quotable (~45 words), one vivid idea. Use a metaphor (Mourinho: "better eggs make better omelets").
  • Bridging — don't dodge a hostile question; acknowledge it, then transition: "I understand — and what's important here is…"
  • Flagging — signal the line you want quoted: "The one thing I'd really highlight is…"
PRLab — Media Training: Control the Narrative

PR myths to kill on a call

"PR is just press releases"

No — it's strategy, relationships, and finding the right story. The release is the smallest part.

"Any publicity is good publicity"

No — the wrong outlet or wrong message can actively hurt. Targeting is everything.

"PR is free because it's earned"

Earned ≠ free. It takes senior time, judgment, and relationships you can't fake.

"We'll just go viral"

Virality is luck, not a strategy. Consistent authority is what compounds.

"AVE proves the ROI"

AVE is discredited industry-wide (Barcelona Principles). Measure outcomes, not ad-equivalents.

"PR works overnight"

It compounds over 3–12 months. Set that expectation and you'll never get the month-1 complaint.

How the pros measure (Barcelona Principles)

The global standard for PR measurement. Drop the name and you sound seasoned. The gist:

  • Set SMART goals before the work — measurement is part of planning, not an afterthought.
  • Measure outputs (placements), outcomes (behavior change), and impact (business result).
  • Use both quantitative and qualitative data.
  • AVE is not the value of communication. Full stop.

Situational Crisis Communication Theory: pick your posture by how much the public blames you. Deny (low blame) → Diminish (excuse/justify) → Rebuild (apologize, compensate), with Bolstering as support. W. Timothy Coombs.

Five ways to restore reputation: denial, evade responsibility, reduce offensiveness, corrective action, mortification (apology). William Benoit.

THE PSYCHOLOGY · COLE GORDON × HORMOZI

Closing & influence — why the method works.

Your v6 Call 1 is built on Cole Gordon's framework. This is the psychology underneath it — plus Alex Hormozi's value math — so you're not just running beats, you understand the levers you're pulling. (Distilled from their public training; see the Library.)

The 7 beliefs every buyer must hold

Cole Gordon: a prospect buys only when all seven are true. Your job in discovery is to make them say each one out loud — Cialdini's consistency bias means they close themselves. And here's the thing: your v6 beats are the 7 beliefs, in order. That's not a coincidence.

1 · Pain

There's a real problem/gap. → Beats 1–2

2 · Money

Resources + willingness exist. → Beat 3

3 · Support

The stakeholders are on board. → Beat 3 / partner

4 · Doubt

They can't fix it alone. → Beat 4

5 · Cost

Inaction costs more than the fee. → Beat 5

6 · Desire

The payoff is compelling & personal. → Beat 6 (NMG)

7 · Trust — in the method (not you personally). This is the one belief you install on Call 2, when you pitch the plan. It's why the close is a separate call.

The objection framework

Cole Gordon: every objection is one of three things — Uncertainty, Finance, or Support. The iron rule: solve Uncertainty first. You can never fix money or a spouse if they're not 100% sure it's the right thing and the right time.

The sequence after you state the price

  • State the price on a downward tone, then go silent. Price only — no discount, no payment plan yet.
  • First words after any objection: "No problem." (kills the pressure / lizard-brain).
  • Isolate with a binary (the lizard brain can't do open-ended): "Are you more in a should-I-do-this place, or a how-can-I-do-this place?"
  • Double tie-down: "So — money aside — you're 100% in?" A soft "yeah, I think so" = hidden uncertainty.
  • Root it out: "Tell me more about what's going on." Only then reframe the one real objection.

Prevention beats rebuttal: answer questions with "Is there a specific reason you're asking?", keep answers to 1–2 sentences, then "Does that make sense? What else?" — and temp-check on a 1–10 ("investment aside, where are you on the process?"). Anything under 9 isn't ready.

The "1 question" that 2×'s closes

It's not a question — it's a format: Permission + a Reason-Why (or Context) + the Question. It makes the prospect justify their own desire to you, so consistency bias does the closing. You already use it twice in v6:

"Can I ask you something personal? What matters to me isn't just your business but the life you want — so what's the non-monetary goal here?"Permission + reason-why → this IS your Beat 6 NMG move
"After 10 years doing it the old way… what changed? What happened?"Permission + context → hunts the moment of decision (your Beat 5 "why now")
"Is there any other reason this matters?"surfaces the real reason vs. the one that sounds good → your Beat 5 double-pump

Tonality — the sale is won at hello

  • Say "feel," not "think" — keep them emotional, not logical.
  • End questions on a downward, concerned tone so they think deeper.
  • Slow right down before the price — then go silent. No rescuing.
  • Lean out to make them lean in. Playful first to disarm; direct once the guard is down.
  • Trust isn't built by small talk — it's built by understanding their problem better than they can.

Hormozi's value equation

Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort)

Raise the top, shrink the bottom. For a PR retainer:

  • Dream outcome ↑ — the Forbes feature, the inbound, the authority (sell that, not "pitches").
  • Likelihood ↑ — your 40 case studies, named outlets, the Edelman data. Proof raises belief.
  • Time delay ↓ — "outreach starts within 2 weeks; first placements 4–12 weeks."
  • Effort ↓ — "we do the heavy lifting; ~2 hours a month from you."

"The most expensive part of an offer isn't the price — it's the hidden costs" (their time and effort). Kill those and the same retainer is worth 10× more.

Three Hormozi lines to sell PR by

"Sell the vacation, not the plane flight."Sell the outcome — authority, inbound, the deal you win — not bylines and media lists.
"Proof does more selling than any promise."Lead with the 40 case studies and real numbers, not adjectives.
"Rich people pay for time above all."Premium price is the signal, not the barrier. Five $5K clients beat fifty $500 ones — and the cheap client nitpicks most.
